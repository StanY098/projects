Aperture

Introduction

Aperture is one of the three basic elements for photographing. The other two elements are ISO and Shutter. Aperture is to check a measurement of the size of the aperture. By checking it, the camera checks on the radius of the aperture, the bigger the radius is, the larger the aperture is. The lens' aperture range is F1 (the largest aperture), F2.8, F4.0, F5.6, all the way up to F22 (the smallest aperture). It depends on a specific lens.

Caution

Please be careful! The camera will show F2.8 as your aperture, for instance. the number 2.8, however, means how thick is between the aperture and the lens. Therefore, the less the number is for the F, the greater aperture is.

The difference between a Small Aperture with a Slow Shutter and a Large Aperture with a Fast Shutter

Aperture and Shutter have a very strong and closed relationship between each other. Having a small aperture with a slow shutter and a large aperture with a fast shutter seems to have the same and appropriate light for a photograph, but there is still a difference between these two settings.

Aperture not only enlarge the amount of light abosrption, but also make background blur. The larger the aperture is, the greater difference between the object and the background (more blur at the background). Shallow depth of field (making background blur so that the object can be more emphasized) is an example to apply this setting.

However, as we take group pictures and want to take clear and plain pictures. A large aperture cannot be applied. Therefore, another setting (a small aperture with a slow shutter) can be better applied. A small aperture can help make a completely clear picture. Even though a slow shutter may cause objects blur because of movement, it is still a small matter for the whole picture, comparing to blur, except it is sporty or for a highly moving activity.

Compare figures 1.5 and 1.6, figure 1.5 with using aperture F3.2 is more blur than figure 1.6 with using aperture F25. The more light the lens receive, the more spread of light the picture will be.

Therefore, to determine it should be a small aperture with a slow shutter or a large aperture with a fast shutter, the type or purpose for a picture is very important, to make it blur or not.

Conclusion

Aperture refers to the size of open lens radius. The smaller the number is, the larger the aperture is. Higher aperture may also make pictures blur. If you still don't have enough light, you can also use an external flash light.

REMEMBER: Low Aperture will make the whole picture clear but receive less light. High Aperture will make the whole picture blur but receive more light.
